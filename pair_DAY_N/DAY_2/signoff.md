**What I understand now that I didn't before:**
Before reading this explainer, I knew the Phase 5 mechanism worked — SOC-01 went from FAIL to PASS — but I could only describe what happened, not why. I wrote "prompt-only constraint proved insufficient" as if that were an explanation, when it was just an observation. Now I understand the load-bearing mechanism: a system-prompt honesty rule is a soft logit influence that shifts the probability distribution over next tokens but does not block any token absolutely. During open-ended generation, the model balances competing objectives simultaneously — write persuasive sales copy, sound relevant, infer company growth, follow the honesty constraint — and when the first three aligned on a high-probability sales phrase, the single honesty rule lost locally. The constraint was present; it just lost a vote it was always going to lose against three stronger priors from the training distribution. The specific insight that closed my gap: the follow-up user turn repair worked for a structurally different reason than "it repeated the rule more forcefully." It collapsed five competing objectives into one concrete task — violation-specific repair — by naming the exact forbidden phrase and labeling it as wrong. The model was no longer doing open-ended generation; it was correcting a labeled mistake, a pattern it has internalized from training. Recency mattered, but recency alone was not the explanation — a recent irrelevant message would not have fixed it. Concreteness and task-narrowing were the mechanism. I can now defend the Phase 5 design not as a Python workaround but as a verifier that converts abstract policy priors into concrete, local, violation-specific repair signals, and I know what any engineer extending the claim-type coverage needs to understand before touching `_ASSERTIVE_CLAIM_RE`: the goal is not to make the system prompt louder, it is to name the violation after generation and make the repair task as narrow as possible.
